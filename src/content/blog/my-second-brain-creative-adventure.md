*And how I found amazing ways Claude could help me in my actual work.*

I'm a tech junkie anyway, so these ideas have always fascinated me. I also waste far too much time exploring them, experimenting, then leaving them behind when they don't serve me in the ways I expected.

Well, I might have discovered — not my discovery, but a *personal* discovery nonetheless — how emerging tech CAN help me in my daily creative work.

And yes, it involves AI.

And yes, I know about all the concerns. I share those concerns. I'd also love to have a debate with you (at another time) about how some of those concerns are misplaced, in my opinion. But we're going to leave that aside for now — AI is here, and it's here to stay. And if we don't figure out thoughtful, moral ways to use it, we might be surprised when it bites us in the ... well .....

I digress.

There are two tech interests I've developed lately: the note-taking app [Obsidian](https://obsidian.md), and the AI agent [Claude](https://claude.ai).

## Building a Second Brain

I've been working on building a "second brain" for some time — first with Notion, which is fantastic, but I found Obsidian more attractive. The idea: one central place where I could store every idea, thought, resource, and plan, all easily accessible, searchable, and discoverable. Obsidian fit the bill.

So I started building and following the practices Obsidian is designed for — linking notes, connecting similar ideas, using tags — so everything is connected.

This is cool! It's also a rather manual process. Tags make things easier, but it's still manual.

Enter Claude.

## Claude as a Creative Collaborator

I'm a relatively new user of Claude, and I've already discovered some amazing uses in my creative work. For example, just recently I was trying to brainstorm ideas for an upcoming cabaret and needed songs from genres I wasn't familiar with. So I just asked Claude:

> "I'm producing a cabaret featuring solo singers called *Songs We Can't Sing on Stage*. It's basically a concert featuring songs that are a bit risqué, funny, slightly sexual, eyebrow-raising, fun, silly — all the things a sophisticated choir wouldn't perform. It will be a casual and fun event. What are some songs can I program? Can range from old songs, to Broadway, to new pop — anything really. Give me some ideas!"

And it delivered a thorough, thoughtful list that genuinely blew me away.

This saved me hours of work. And importantly, it didn't *do* the creative work for me — it only introduced me to things I didn't already know. That's a distinction I care about a lot.

## When It All Clicked

Later that same day, I came across a video about building an AI second brain using Obsidian and Claude together. Things just *clicked*.

I followed the instructions, and in under an hour — after feeding Claude only **two articles**, just two — Claude had built a TTBB Repertoire Wiki for me inside Obsidian. It will only grow as I add content. I simply ask Claude to add a new article to my wiki and it does the work: creating connections, links, descriptions, everything.

You can see the result here: [Sean Baugh Notes — Obsidian Publish](https://publish.obsidian.md/seanbaugh/03+-+WIKI/index)

That's from just two articles.

## The Part That Really Got Me

Then things got even more interesting. I asked Claude simply: *"What pieces in my wiki would be good for my upcoming September 11 concert?"*

And it gave me a detailed, thoughtful response drawing directly from my own notes.

I'm dumbfounded.

But also genuinely excited — because one of the most difficult tasks I face as a concert programmer is keeping track of potential repertoire. Pieces I've encountered over years, half-remembered titles, articles I bookmarked and forgot. This system has the potential to change all of that.

Will this experiment eventually get cast onto the heap of fun projects I've abandoned? Maybe.

But you cannot deny how powerful and useful this can be for creatives. And the best part: it's not doing the creative work *for* us — it's giving us better tools to do our work more efficiently.

I'll report back with more discoveries.
